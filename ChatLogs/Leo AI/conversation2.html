User: You are a personal injury lawyer working in Nashville. A client was partially responsible for a car accident but wants to sue. What information do you need to know?
LLM: Key Information Needed for Partially Responsible Client's Case:
  • Accident details: Date, time, location, and a brief description of the accident, including any injuries sustained.
  • Police report: Obtain a copy of the police report filed by the responding officer.
  • Insurance information: Gather insurance details for both parties involved, including policy numbers and coverage limits.
  • Witness statements: Collect statements from any witnesses who may have seen the accident.
  • Medical records: Obtain medical records and bills related to any injuries sustained in the accident.
  • Prior medical conditions: Disclosure of any pre-existing medical conditions that may be relevant to the case.
  • Vehicle damage: Photos and documentation of the damage to both vehicles involved in the accident.
  • Other relevant information: Any other details that may be relevant to the case, such as traffic citations or previous accidents.
Additionally, I'll need to understand the client's proposed theory of liability and how they plan to allocate fault between the parties involved.

Tennessee Comparative Negligence Law:

In Tennessee, we follow a comparative negligence law, where the plaintiff's recovery is reduced by their percentage of fault. This means that if your client is found to be partially responsible for the accident, their damages will be reduced accordingly.

User: How does Tennessee's modified comparative fault rule affect this case?
LLM: Tennessee's Modified Comparative Fault Rule:

In Tennessee, we follow a modified comparative fault rule, which states that a plaintiff can recover damages only if they are not more than 49% at fault for the accident. If the plaintiff is found to be 50% or more at fault, they are barred from recovering any damages.

Impact on the Case:
  • If the court finds your client to be 30% at fault, they can recover 70% of their damages.
  • If the court finds your client to be 50% at fault, they are barred from recovering any damages.

We will carefully review the evidence and present a strong case to minimize your client's percentage of fault and maximize their potential recovery.

User: If the client is found 40% at fault, what happens to the damages awarded?
LLM: Damages Reduction in Tennessee:

If your client is found to be 40% at fault, their damages will be reduced by 40%.

For example, if the court awards $100,000 in damages:

Reduced Damages = $100,000 x (1 - 0.40) = $60,000

Strategies for Maximizing Recovery:
  • Challenging the opposing party's liability.
  • Presenting evidence of the opposing party's negligence.
  • Highlighting any contributory negligence on the opposing party's part.
  • Arguing that the opposing party's actions were more egregious than your client's.

LLM: Tennessee's 50% Bar to Recovery:

Yes, in Tennessee, if the plaintiff is found to be 50% or more at fault for the accident, they are barred from recovering any damages.

Impact of 50% or More Fault:
  • If the court finds your client to be 51% at fault, they are barred from recovering any damages.
  • If the court finds your client to be 75% at fault, they are still barred from recovering any damages.
